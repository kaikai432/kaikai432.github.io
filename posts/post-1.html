The quiet cost of always being reachable

There's a version of this essay I wrote in my head while staring at my phone at 11pm on a Tuesday. I'd already put it face-down twice. Both times, the buzz of a notification pulled it back up.

We talk a lot about screen time, about doomscrolling, about social media. But I think the more insidious problem is simpler: we've made ourselves permanently reachable, and we've never stopped to ask what that costs.

The always-on expectation

When email became mobile, the tacit contract of work changed. An email sent at 9pm used to wait until morning. Now it arrives in your pocket, and the social norm has quietly shifted — a reply before bed is expected, or at least noticed when absent.

The problem isn't that we're distracted. It's that we've made distraction the default state, and focus the thing that requires effort.
